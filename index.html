Welcome to the abyss. Unlike the first two Niche Integrals, this is not a reverse quotient rule or a deflated definite integral. This is a pure, forward indefinite integral that requires seven distinct advanced integration strategies. It weaves Complex Analysis, Euler Sums, Leibniz's Rule for Differential Equations, and obscure Special Functions into a single problem. Strategy 1 & 2: Contour Integration & Fresnel Miracles (The Constant $\mathcal{C}$)

The entire first bracketed fraction is a monolithic constant, $\mathcal{C}$. We evaluate it piece by piece:

1. The Contour Integral: $\int_0^\infty \frac{\cos t}{t^2+1} dt$
Using the Residue Theorem on a semi-circular contour in the upper half-plane for $f(z) = \frac{e^{iz}}{z^2+1}$, we find a simple pole at $z=i$. The residue evaluates to $\frac{e^{-1}}{2i}$. Multiplying by $2\pi i$ and taking half (since it's an even function) yields exactly $\frac{\pi}{2e}$.

2. The Fresnel Integral: $\int_0^\infty \sin(t^2) dt$
By integrating $e^{-z^2}$ over a pie-slice contour of angle $\pi/4$, we bypass standard real analysis to find this equals $\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{8}}$.

3. The Euler Sum: $\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{H_n}{n^2}$
This is a famous non-linear Euler sum involving Harmonic numbers, known to exactly equal $2\zeta(3)$.

Let's compile the constant $\mathcal{C}$:

$$ \mathcal{C} = \frac{ 4\sqrt{8} \cdot \left(\frac{\pi}{2e}\right) \cdot \left(\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{8}}\right) }{ \pi^{3/2} e^{-1} \frac{1}{\zeta(3)} \cdot 2\zeta(3) } = \frac{ 2\pi^{3/2} e^{-1} }{ 2\pi^{3/2} e^{-1} } = 1 $$

The massive, terrifying constant coefficient is an intricate disguise for the number $1$.

Strategy 3 & 4: Feynman's Trick & ODEs (The Dynamic Canceller)

Now observe the second bracket. It contains an integral function: $g(u) = \int_0^\infty \frac{\cos(tu)}{1+t^2} dt$, where $u = \operatorname{arcosh}(x)$.

Instead of Contour Integration, we use Leibniz's Rule (Feynman's Trick) to form a Differential Equation. Differentiating twice with respect to $u$:

$$ g''(u) = \int_0^\infty \frac{-t^2\cos(tu)}{1+t^2} dt = \int_0^\infty \frac{(1 - (1+t^2))\cos(tu)}{1+t^2} dt = g(u) - \int_0^\infty \cos(tu) dt $$

The term $\int_0^\infty \cos(tu) dt$ behaves as a Dirac delta function $\pi \delta(u)$. For $u > 0$ (since $x>1$), it vanishes. We get the ODE:

$$ g''(u) - g(u) = 0 \implies g(u) = c_1 e^u + c_2 e^{-u} $$

Applying boundary conditions $g(0) = \pi/2$ and $\lim_{u \to \infty} g(u) = 0$, we find $g(u) = \frac{\pi}{2} e^{-u}$.

Substituting this back into the second bracket: $\frac{2}{\pi} e^{u} \cdot \left(\frac{\pi}{2} e^{-u}\right) = 1$. The entire second term beautifully obliterates itself!

Strategy 5: The Hyperbolic Core

The integrand has now shed its armor, revealing its true, stubborn core:

$$ \mathcal{I} = \int \frac{\operatorname{arcosh}(x)}{x \sqrt{x^2-1}} \, dx $$

We execute a hyperbolic substitution. Let $u = \operatorname{arcosh}(x)$, which means $x = \cosh(u)$ and $dx = \sinh(u) du$. The term $\sqrt{x^2-1}$ becomes $\sinh(u)$.

$$ \mathcal{I} = \int \frac{u}{\cosh(u) \sinh(u)} \sinh(u) \, du = \int \frac{u}{\cosh(u)} \, du = \int u \operatorname{sech}(u) \, du $$

Strategy 6: Exponential Substitution & Integration by Parts

To conquer $\int u \operatorname{sech}(u) du$, we expand the secant into its exponential form: $\int \frac{2u}{e^u + e^{-u}} du = \int \frac{2u e^u}{e^{2u} + 1} du$.

Let $w = e^u$, so $dw = e^u du$ and $u = \ln(w)$. This transforms the integral into:

$$ \int \frac{2 \ln(w)}{w^2 + 1} \, dw $$

We attack this with Integration by Parts. Let $A = \ln(w)$ and $dB = \frac{2}{w^2+1} dw$. Then $dA = \frac{1}{w} dw$ and $B = 2\arctan(w)$.

$$ \mathcal{I} = 2\ln(w)\arctan(w) - 2\int \frac{\arctan(w)}{w} \, dw $$

Strategy 7: Special Functions (The Inverse Tangent Integral)

The final remaining integral, $\int \frac{\arctan(w)}{w} dw$, does not possess an elementary closed form. It is the literal definition of the Inverse Tangent Integral, denoted as $\operatorname{Ti}_2(w)$, which is deeply related to Complex Dilogarithms via $\operatorname{Ti}_2(z) = \frac{1}{2i} [ \operatorname{Li}_2(iz) - \operatorname{Li}_2(-iz) ]$.

Thus, our expression evaluates to:

$$ 2\ln(w)\arctan(w) - 2\operatorname{Ti}_2(w) + C $$

Final Closed Form

We reverse our substitutions: $w = e^u$ and $u = \operatorname{arcosh}(x)$. Noting that $e^{\operatorname{arcosh}(x)} = x + \sqrt{x^2-1}$, we arrive at the absolute masterclass final answer:

$$ 2\operatorname{arcosh}(x) \arctan\left(x + \sqrt{x^2-1}\right) - 2\operatorname{Ti}_2\left(x + \sqrt{x^2-1}\right) + C $$
