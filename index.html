$${ \int \frac{ \left[ x^{x^x} \cdot x^x \left( \ln x(1+\ln x) + \frac{1}{x} \right) \cdot \Gamma(\operatorname{Si}(x)) \cdot \operatorname{W}_0(\phi^x) + x^{x^x} \cdot \Gamma(\operatorname{Si}(x)) \psi^{(0)}(\operatorname{Si}(x)) \frac{\sin x \operatorname{W}_0(\phi^x)}{x} + \frac{x^{x^x} \Gamma(\operatorname{Si}(x)) \operatorname{W}_0(\phi^x) \ln \phi}{1 + \operatorname{W}_0(\phi^x)} \right] \cdot \left( \operatorname{erf}(\sqrt{x}) + \operatorname{Li}_2\left(\frac{1}{1+x^2}\right) \right) - \left( x^{x^x} \Gamma(\operatorname{Si}(x)) \operatorname{W}_0(\phi^x) \right) \cdot \left( \frac{e^{-x}}{\sqrt{\pi x}} + \frac{2x \ln\left( \frac{x^2}{1+x^2} \right)}{1+x^2} \right) }{ \left( \operatorname{erf}(\sqrt{x}) + \operatorname{Li}_2\left(\frac{1}{1+x^2}\right) \right)^2 } \, dx }$$

At first glance, this integral appears to be a chaotic collection of transcendental functions. However, the structure is a precisely engineered application of the Quotient Rule in reverse.

The fundamental identity being used is:

$$\int \frac{u'(x)v(x) - u(x)v'(x)}{[v(x)]^2} \, dx = \frac{u(x)}{v(x)} + C$$

Step 1: Identify the Parent Functions

We define the numerator function $u(x)$ and the denominator function $v(x)$ from the integrand:

Numerator: $$u(x) = x^{x^x} \cdot \Gamma(\operatorname{Si}(x)) \cdot \operatorname{W}_0(\phi^x)$$
Denominator: $$v(x) = \operatorname{erf}(\sqrt{x}) + \operatorname{Li}_2\left(\frac{1}{1+x^2}\right)$$

Step 2: Differentiate the Numerator \(u'(x)\)

Applying the Product Rule to $u(x)$ requires three separate derivatives:

  • Tower of Power: $\frac{d}{dx}(x^{x^x}) = x^{x^x} \cdot x^x \left( \ln x(1+\ln x) + \frac{1}{x} \right)$
  • Gamma of Sine Integral: $\frac{d}{dx}\Gamma(\operatorname{Si}(x)) = \Gamma(\operatorname{Si}(x)) \psi^{(0)}(\operatorname{Si}(x)) \frac{\sin x}{x}$
  • Lambert W Function: $\frac{d}{dx}\operatorname{W}_0(\phi^x) = \frac{\operatorname{W}_0(\phi^x) \ln \phi}{1 + \operatorname{W}_0(\phi^x)}$

Combining these yields the massive bracketed expression in the first half of the integral's numerator.

Step 3: Differentiate the Denominator \(v'(x)\)

Differentiating $v(x)$ involves the Error Function and the Dilogarithm:

  • Error Function: $\frac{d}{dx}\operatorname{erf}(\sqrt{x}) = \frac{e^{-x}}{\sqrt{\pi x}}$
  • Dilogarithm: $\frac{d}{dx}\operatorname{Li}_2\left(\frac{1}{1+x^2}\right) = \frac{2x \ln\left( \frac{x^2}{1+x^2} \right)}{1+x^2}$

This matches the subtracted term in the second half of the integral's numerator.

Final Result

Since the integrand is exactly $\frac{d}{dx} [u(x)/v(x)]$, the solution is simply the ratio of our two parent functions:

$$\frac{ x^{x^x} \cdot \Gamma(\operatorname{Si}(x)) \cdot \operatorname{W}_0(\phi^x) }{ \operatorname{erf}(\sqrt{x}) + \operatorname{Li}_2\left(\frac{1}{1+x^2}\right) } + C$$

$${ \int_{0}^{\infty} \frac{ \ln(1+x^2) \cdot \left[ \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{ (\cos x)^{2k} e^{-\cos^2 x} }{ k! } \right] \cdot \left[ \int_{0}^{\infty} J_1(t) \, dt \right] }{ (x^4 + x^2) \cdot \left[ \frac{\cosh(\pi x)}{\pi} \left| \Gamma\left(\frac{1}{2} + ix\right) \right|^2 \right] } \, dx }$$

This monstrous definite integral seems designed to break standard calculus, throwing probability distributions, infinite series, Bessel functions, and complex Gamma properties into a single blender. However, it is fundamentally a Trojan Horse. Once the illusions are stripped away, it reveals a stubborn core that demands a brilliant combination of Feynman's Trick and Contour Integration.

Step 1: Defusing the Illusions (Simplification)

Almost everything in this integrand is a highly obfuscated form of the number $1$. Let's break down the distractions:

1. The Probability / Sigma Sum:
The term $\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{ (\cos x)^{2k} e^{-\cos^2 x} }{ k! }$ is exactly the sum over all possible outcomes $k$ of a Poisson Distribution with parameter $\lambda = \cos^2 x$. Because the sum of all probabilities in a distribution must equal $1$, this entire series collapses perfectly to $1$.

2. The Bessel Integral:
A well-known identity for the Bessel function of the first kind states that $\int_0^\infty J_\nu(t) \, dt = 1$ for $\Re(\nu) > -1$. Thus, $\int_0^\infty J_1(t) \, dt = 1$.

3. The Euler Reflection Formula:
In the denominator, we have the squared magnitude of the complex Gamma function. By Euler's reflection formula, $\left| \Gamma\left(\frac{1}{2} + ix\right) \right|^2 = \frac{\pi}{\cosh(\pi x)}$. Multiplying this by $\frac{\cosh(\pi x)}{\pi}$ clearly cancels out to $1$.

Factoring $x^4 + x^2 = x^2(1+x^2)$, our nightmare integral gracefully reduces to its true, stubbornly difficult core:

$$\mathcal{I} = \int_{0}^{\infty} \frac{\ln(1+x^2)}{x^2(1+x^2)} \, dx$$

Step 2: Feynman's Trick (Differentiation Under the Integral Sign)

To evaluate this, we introduce a parameter $\alpha$ into the argument of the natural log:

$$I(\alpha) = \int_{0}^{\infty} \frac{\ln(1+\alpha^2 x^2)}{x^2(1+x^2)} \, dx$$

Our goal is to find $I(1)$. We differentiate $I(\alpha)$ with respect to $\alpha$ (using the Leibniz Rule):

$$I'(\alpha) = \frac{\partial}{\partial \alpha} \int_{0}^{\infty} \frac{\ln(1+\alpha^2 x^2)}{x^2(1+x^2)} \, dx = \int_{0}^{\infty} \frac{2\alpha x^2}{x^2(1+x^2)(1+\alpha^2 x^2)} \, dx$$

The $x^2$ terms brilliantly cancel out, yielding:

$$I'(\alpha) = 2\alpha \int_{0}^{\infty} \frac{1}{(1+x^2)(1+\alpha^2 x^2)} \, dx$$

Step 3: Contour Integration (The Residue Theorem)

To evaluate the resulting sub-integral $J(\alpha) = \int_{0}^{\infty} \frac{1}{(1+x^2)(1+\alpha^2 x^2)} \, dx$, we can use the Residue Theorem. Because the integrand is an even function, we extend the bounds to $(-\infty, \infty)$ and divide by $2$.

Consider the complex function $f(z) = \frac{1}{(z^2+1)(\alpha^2 z^2+1)}$ integrated over a standard semi-circular contour in the upper half-plane. Assuming $\alpha > 0$, the poles inside the contour are at $z = i$ and $z = \frac{i}{\alpha}$.

Residue at $z = i$:
$\operatorname{Res}(f, i) = \lim_{z \to i} \frac{z-i}{(z-i)(z+i)(\alpha^2 z^2+1)} = \frac{1}{2i(1-\alpha^2)}$

Residue at $z = i/\alpha$:
$\operatorname{Res}\left(f, \frac{i}{\alpha}\right) = \lim_{z \to i/\alpha} \frac{z-i/\alpha}{(z^2+1)\alpha^2(z-i/\alpha)(z+i/\alpha)} = \frac{1}{ ( (i/\alpha)^2 + 1 )\alpha^2(2i/\alpha) } = \frac{-\alpha}{2i(1-\alpha^2)}$

Summing the residues and applying the Residue Theorem $2\pi i \sum \operatorname{Res}$:

$$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x) \, dx = 2\pi i \left( \frac{1-\alpha}{2i(1-\alpha^2)} \right) = 2\pi i \left( \frac{1-\alpha}{2i(1-\alpha)(1+\alpha)} \right) = \frac{\pi}{1+\alpha}$$

Since $J(\alpha)$ is half of this, $J(\alpha) = \frac{\pi}{2(1+\alpha)}$. Substituting this back into $I'(\alpha)$ gives us:

$$I'(\alpha) = 2\alpha \cdot \frac{\pi}{2(1+\alpha)} = \frac{\pi \alpha}{1+\alpha}$$

Step 4: The Final Descent (Integrating Back)

We now integrate $I'(\alpha)$ with respect to $\alpha$ to recover our function $I(\alpha)$:

$$I(\alpha) = \int \frac{\pi \alpha}{1+\alpha} \, d\alpha = \pi \int \left( 1 - \frac{1}{1+\alpha} \right) \, d\alpha = \pi \Big( \alpha - \ln(1+\alpha) \Big) + C$$

To find $C$, we observe from our original parameterization that $I(0) = 0$. Plugging in $\alpha = 0$ yields $0 = \pi(0 - \ln 1) + C \implies C = 0$.

Finally, we evaluate $I(\alpha)$ at our target value $\alpha = 1$:

$$I(1) = \pi(1 - \ln 2)$$
